General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)

The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) is a comprehensive data protection law that came into effect in the European Union (EU) in May 2018. It replaces the 1995 EU Data Protection Directive and applies to all EU member states. The GDPR applies to organizations operating within the EU, as well as organizations outside of the EU that offer goods or services to individuals within the EU or that monitor the behavior of individuals within the EU.

The GDPR sets out strict rules for the collection, storage, and use of personal data, and gives individuals significant new rights in relation to their personal data. Some key features of the GDPR include:

  • It applies to both controllers (organizations that determine the purposes and means of processing personal data) and processors (organizations that process personal data on behalf of controllers).

  • It requires organizations to obtain explicit, informed consent from individuals for the collection and use of their personal data.

  • It gives individuals the right to access their personal data, the right to have their personal data corrected or deleted, and the right to data portability (meaning they can request a copy of their data in a commonly used format).

  • It requires organizations to appoint a Data Protection Officer (DPO) if they are a public body or if their core activities involve regular and systematic monitoring of individuals or large-scale processing of special categories of data (such as sensitive personal data).

  • It requires organizations to report data breaches to the relevant supervisory authority and in certain cases to the affected individuals,

  • It imposes significant fines for non-compliance, up to €20 million or 4% of the company's global annual revenue, whichever is higher.

The GDPR is considered as one of the strictest data protection laws in the world, and it has set a benchmark for many other countries and regions to follow.

California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA)

The California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) is a comprehensive data protection law that came into effect in California, USA, in January 2020. It applies to all organizations that collect, sell, or share the personal information of California residents, regardless of where the organization is based.

The CCPA gives California residents certain rights with respect to their personal information, including:

  • The right to know what personal information a business has collected about them and how it is being used.

  • The right to request that a business delete any personal information about them that the business has collected.

  • The right to opt-out of the sale of their personal information.

  • The right not to be discriminated against for exercising their rights under the CCPA.

The CCPA requires businesses to disclose certain information to California residents upon request, including:

  • The categories of personal information the business has collected about the resident in the past 12 months.

  • The categories of sources from which the information was collected.

  • The business or commercial purpose for collecting or selling the information.

  • The categories of third parties with whom the information was shared.

Businesses must also provide a way for California residents to make requests to know and requests to delete, and must provide notice of this right to California residents on their website.

The CCPA also requires businesses to implement reasonable security measures to protect personal information and to appoint a qualified individual as a data protection officer. It also authorized the attorney general to enforce the law, and for individuals to bring private actions for certain data breaches.

It's worth noting that California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA) was passed in November 2020, amending the CCPA, and set to be effective from 2023. This new law will impose more strict regulations on businesses, and will introduce new rights for California consumers.

Children's Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA)

The Children's Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA) is a federal law in the United States that regulates the collection, use, and disclosure of personal information from children under the age of 13. The law was enacted in 1998 and is enforced by the Federal Trade Commission (FTC).

COPPA applies to online operators (websites, mobile apps, and other online services) that are directed to children under 13, and operators that have actual knowledge that they are collecting personal information from children under 13. The law applies to both domestic and foreign operators that are doing business in the United States.

COPPA requires online operators to:

  • Provide notice to parents and obtain their verifiable consent before collecting, using, or disclosing personal information from children under 13.

  • Provide parents with a choice as to whether their child’s personal information can be used for certain purposes (such as marketing or third-party sharing)

  • Implement reasonable procedures to protect the confidentiality, security, and integrity of personal information collected from children.

  • Retain personal information collected from children only as long as is necessary to fulfill the purpose for which it was collected, and then delete it.

COPPA also requires online operators to provide parents with access to the personal information collected from their child, and the ability to have it deleted.

It is important to note that COPPA is a federal law and that some states have their own laws that may provide additional protections for children's personal information. Also, COPPA has been updated in 2019 to include provisions to address the rapidly evolving child-directed technology landscape.

Electronic Communications Privacy Act (ECPA)

The Electronic Communications Privacy Act (ECPA) is a federal law in the United States that regulates government access to electronic communications and stored data. The law was enacted in 1986 and has been amended several times since.

The ECPA regulates government access to:

  • Wire and electronic communications in transit, such as phone calls and emails.

  • Stored communications, such as emails and text messages that are stored on servers.

  • Electronic communications and stored data held by third-party service providers, such as internet service providers (ISPs) and cloud storage providers.

The ECPA requires the government to obtain a warrant based on probable cause in order to access wire and electronic communications in transit, which are considered to have a high expectation of privacy. For stored communications, the ECPA sets out different standards for access depending on the age of the communication and whether it has been opened.

For example, for unopened stored communications that are less than 180 days old, the government must obtain a warrant based on probable cause. For opened communications or communications that are more than 180 days old, the government can obtain access through a subpoena or court order that is issued upon a showing of relevance and materiality to an ongoing criminal investigation.

The ECPA also allows the government to obtain certain types of electronic communications and stored data without a warrant or court order under certain circumstances, such as in an emergency situation or with the consent of the user.

The ECPA has been criticized for being outdated and not reflecting the current state of technology and the way people use electronic communications. Some lawmakers and advocacy groups have called for the law to be updated to provide stronger privacy protections.

Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA)

The Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA) is a federal law in the United States that criminalizes unauthorized access to computer systems. The law was enacted in 1986 and has been amended several times since.

The CFAA makes it a crime to:

  • Intentionally access a computer without authorization or exceed authorized access.

  • Intentionally cause damage to a computer or the information it stores.

  • Traffic in stolen computer passwords or other information that can be used to access a computer without authorization.

  • Use a computer to commit fraud.

  • Attempt or conspire to commit any of the above offenses.

Laws specific to Sweden

Sweden has several laws specific to data protection and privacy. Some examples include:

  • The Personal Data Act (PDA) is the main legislation governing data protection in Sweden. It regulates the processing of personal data, including the collection, storage, use, and disclosure of such data. The PDA requires organizations to obtain the consent of individuals for the processing of their personal data, and also requires organizations to take appropriate technical and organizational measures to protect personal data.

  • The Electronic Communications Act (ECA) regulates the use of electronic communications services, such as telephone and internet services, and also deals with data protection issues related to electronic communications. The ECA requires telecommunications companies to obtain the consent of individuals for the processing of their personal data, and also requires companies to take appropriate technical and organizational measures to protect personal data.

  • The Patient Data Act regulates the handling of patient data in the healthcare sector. It sets out strict rules for the collection, storage, and use of patient data, and gives patients significant rights in relation to their personal data.

  • The Public Access to Information and Secrecy Act regulates the right of access to official documents and the secrecy surrounding certain types of information held by public authorities.

  • The Marketing Act regulates the use of personal data for marketing purposes. It requires companies to obtain consent from individuals before using their personal data for marketing purposes and also requires companies to give individuals the right to opt out of receiving marketing communications.

It is important to note that these laws are subject to change and it is important to stay updated with the current regulations. Additionally, Sweden is part of the European Union and thus, the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) also applies.
